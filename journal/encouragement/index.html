Encouragement design

Renata | Nov 14, 2022

Let’s learn about encouragement design (the idea of randomizing an encouragement to take up a treatment rather than the treatment itself) and the advantages and disadvantages of developing one.

Learning Journey

  1. Understand the theory:
  • We will learn what are encouragement designs and when to use them
  1. Use it in practice:
  • We will learn how to structure an encouragement design

An intro to encouragement design

Encouragement designs are a specific type of research design that can be used when randomized assignment to treatment vs. control is impossible, unethical or impractical (otherwise, undesirable). In it, we don’t randomize treatment, but rather an encouragement (such as a training or a marketing campaign) to be sent out to part of the population.

Reasons for pursuing an encouragement design

  1. You are interested in learning about the effect of the marketing/encouragement being presented (how much more likely is the population that receives the encouragement to take up the program)
  2. You cannot control for confounding variables (observed and unobserved) - if you can control for them, other methods might be better (such as matching)
  3. Encouragement design works, even when you lack data on confounding variables.

Breaking down the concepts used in an encouragement design

There are four types of people in an encouragement design:

  • Always takers: if they get encouragement, they get treatment; if they don’t get the encouragement, then they ALSO get the treatment… some studies do not have always takers. there are situations in which you are not eligible to get the treatment unless you received the encouragement.
  • Never takers: whether or not they get the encouragement, they do not get the treatment. No treatment, no matter what.
  • Compliers: They take the treatment if and only if they get encouraged.
  • Defiers: They take the treatment if and only if they DO NOT get encouraged.

They lead us to four different types of situations: 1. Encouraged and treated: either always takers or compliers 2. Encouraged and not-treated: either never takers or defiers 3. Not encouraged and treated: either always takers or compliers 4. Not encouraged and not-treated: either never takers or compliers

Our focus is on the compliers. As a basic assumption, we rule out the defiers.

The estimates we can get from encouragement designs

ITT: intention to treat effect –> Effect of encouragement on outcomes

LATE: local average treatment effect –> Effect of treatment on outcomes for the compliers

Disadvantages in pursuing encouragement designs

(a) its estimates are actually biased (with bias shrinking as sample size and encouragement effectiveness increase) (b) it only gives you the for the compliers and you never are able to identify all compliers. Best case, you can identify the ones who get the treatment.

Conclusion

Encouragement designs are an option in cases where we cannot randomize treatment, as it allows us to randomize the encouragement to access it. The results we achieve are biased, so they work better with large sample sizes.
