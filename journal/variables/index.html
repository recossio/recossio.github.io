Understanding Variables

Renata | Jul 12, 2022

Let’s learn how to interpret variable relationships (whether they are independent, dependent and confounding).

Learning Journey

  1. Define and operationalize a variable for analysis:
  • We will identify a variable, its type and whether it’s a total, proportion, or average.
  • We will list the units it should be measured in.
  1. Interpret variable relationships:
  • We will structure scenarios in which the variable could be independent or dependent.
  • We will think of possible confounding factors.

Identifying and classifying measurable variables

An important initial step in analyzing a problem, model, or system, is to evaluate the relevant variables - the features that can change. To do so, one must identify and classify the relevant variables, examine the relationships between them, and consider how they can be measured and manipulated. In the context of a study or experiment, this typically involves:

  • Identifying which features play the role of independent variables - the hypothesized predictors or causes - and which play the role of the dependent variable - the hypothesized effect that results from changes in the independent variable(s).
  • Classifying the type of variables is essential as it will affect the type of statistical analyses that are appropriate. Representing a problem formally may also involve identifying the quantities that are constant (not varying), usually referred to as parameters. Parameters help to define the nature of the problem and can be treated as constraints on the solution. We can have at least two types of variables:
Variable type Description
Categorical Classifications, categories (non-ordinal)
Quantitative Discrete set of values that show specific amounts (numbers)

For the kind of relationship analysis we are currently proposing, our focus is on the second type (quantitative).

  • Careful thought must also be given to confounding variables that could also affect the relationship but are not intentionally under study, considering how to control for them (keep them constant).

Brief considerations

Considering the following equation:

Y = mX + b

We assume that:

  • An independent variable is the one that is AFFECTING the dependent variable (the X).
  • A dependent variable, on the other hand, would be the one AFFECTED by the independent variable (the Y).
  • Confounding variables are unmeasured factors that may AFFECT BOTH the independent and the dependent variables.

Our main assumption for this analysis is linear and additive: a single change in variable X (independent) represents an m times change in the variable Y (dependent).

Putting the concepts into the linear function:

  • Y = f(x) = our function of Xs, called predictors or independent variables.
  • dependent variable = “left-hand side variable”
  • independent variable = “right hand side variable(s)”
  • parameters = the slope (m) and the intercept (b) help define the relationship.
Breaking down the concepts

It helps to put the variables in terms of questions. If we wonder about the relationship between number of posts and number of likes on a social media account, questions we could ask include:

  • Does the number of likes AFFECT the number of posts?

In which case the dependent variable is the number of posts and the independent variable is number of likes - but we could also invert the order:

  • Does the number of posts AFFECT the number of likes?

In which case we turn number of posts into the independent variable and number of likes into the dependent.

Interpreting variable relationships

Selected example

Here, we will briefly analyze the variable “investments in Brazilian healthtechs”. We are considering it to be:

  1. Quantitative: money.
  2. Measurable: the total amount invested in the country per year.

Relationship scenarios

Scenario 1: “Investments in Brazilian healthtechs” as the independent variable (X) - is X affected by Y?
  • Possible dependent variables:
  1. Innovation: the expansion of healthtech investments in the country affects the levels of innovation the industry develops (measured by proxy as the number of Research and Development projects, for example).
  2. Healthcare landscape: the expansion of healthtech investments in the country affects its healthcare landscape, with more people changing from public healthcare to the private or hybrid sectors (measured by the percentage of client growth).
  • Possible confounding variables
  1. For inovation, major health emergencies, such as endemics or pandemics, could temporarily skew the industry’s money toward specific types of R&D projects, regardless of the general industry investments (which could also be increasing due to the same reason).
  2. For the healthcare landscape, the lack of public policies or government investments could be making the public turn to other options. In this case the increased demand would be the result of inneficient public health, which could also be part of the reason the private area is getting more investments.
Scenario 2: “Investments in Brazilian healthtechs” as the dependent variable (Y) - is Y affected by X?
  • Possible independent variable:
  1. Investor interest: the expansion of healthtech investments in the country is affected by the level of interest the industry generates. Market growth could be a proxy to measure this effect.
  • Possible confounding variable
  1. External economic factors may be driving the investor interest, not necessarily market growth (which may also be a consequence of said factors).

Conclusion

Our discussion is looking at possible CAUSAL relationships, keeping in mind that association is NOT causation. Interpreting relationships a quantifiable variable might have with others through different scenarios, we recognize that the possible presence of confounding variables - unmeasured elements that can be influencing the relationships - makes it hard for us to gather conclusions without sufficient evidence to confirm them.
