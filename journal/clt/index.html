CLT & Sampling distributions

Renata | Oct 15, 2022

Let’s learn how the central limit theorem relates to the idea of sampling distribution and how we can use simulations in R to model the sampling distributions we need in order to make inferences about a population.

Learning Journey

  • We will learn how the central limit theorem relates to sampling distributions
  1. Use it in practice:

An intro to sampling distributions

There are three levels of analysis for distributions:

  1. Population Distribution: Original population (universe) of the data, which we cannot capture entirely in our data (we can’t necessarily get information on an entire city, for example)
  2. Sample Distribution: a subset of the population distribution, ideally a representative (random) sample (we can get data from a % of the city population, for example)
  3. Sampling Distribution: a distribution drawn from taking repeated samples of the sample distribution

How can we apply these levels of analysis?

Considering a population of interest, we can draw parameters - truths about the population - such as mean, standard deviation, etc. by taking a random sample size n from the population to estimate them. We must keep in mind, though, that those may not necessarily the same as the actual population - i.e., we can take another random sample and they may differ. To mitigate this, we can:

  • Retrieve a sample from the population of interest
  • Simulate repeated samples from this original sample
  • Make an analysis based on the distribution derived from our simulated samples

How does the Central Limit Theorem relate to this?

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is a statistical premise that helps us create/model the sampling distributions that we need, because under its conditions, sampling distributions of sample means are considered to be Normal - that is, if the CLT assumptions hold, they allow us to make assertions regarding the entire population distribution.

What are the CLT conditions?

  • The samples are random
  • The samples are independent (one sample does not affect any other)
  • The samples are of 10% of the population at most
  • The sample sizes are of 30 units or more

CLT for sampling distributions in practice

Let’s say we are analyzing a dataset on start and mid-career salaries by college, region and academic major in the United States, available at kaggle.

How can we prepare the data so that we can make inferences regarding populations from it?

To ensure that the CLT conditions hold, we must pick a sample that:

  • Has at least 30 units
  • Is selected at random
  • Is less than 10% of the entire population

By focusing on the salaries of schools that offer liberal arts majors (LAM) and assuming that the main sample was collected at random and represents less than 10% of the population, we fulfill the CLT conditions given that the LAM schools contain 47 units.

R simulations for sampling means

Given that the Central Limit Theorem conditions hold, we can run simulations that store the sampling means for samples to construct a sampling distribution of the sample mean. This ensures that the distributions could be considered independent, random and normal, regardless of the samples’ original shapes. Here is an example of coding that does just that:

Once we have ensured that the CLT assumptions hold for our selected sample, we can create simulations using R to structure a sampling distribution of sample means and make analyses for which Normal conditions apply.
